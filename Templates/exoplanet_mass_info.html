Understanding Exoplanet Mass

What mass means and how we estimate it
The mass of an exoplanet is a measure of how much matter the planet contains and is usually expressed in units of Earth masses or Jupiter masses. Because individual exoplanets are far too distant to place on a scale, their masses are inferred indirectly from how strongly they interact with their host stars. A planet’s gravity tugs on the star, causing small motions that can be detected in the star’s spectrum (radial velocity method), or it affects the timing and shape of the star’s dimming when the planet passes in front of it (transit timing and transit depth methods). By combining these effects with Kepler’s laws of orbital motion, astronomers can estimate the planet’s mass from the observed orbital period, the size of the orbit, and the star’s properties.
In practice, many exoplanets have only partial information available. Transit surveys measure the planet radius very precisely from the amount of starlight blocked during a transit, but do not directly measure mass. Radial velocity surveys, on the other hand, are more sensitive to massive planets in close orbits and often provide a quantity known as the minimum mass (because the true mass depends on the unknown orbital inclination). For some systems, both techniques are available, allowing astronomers to place planets on a mass–radius diagram and study how different compositions—rocky, icy, or gas-dominated—populate distinct regions of this space. These measurements have revealed empirical relationships between mass, radius, and other orbital parameters for large populations of exoplanets.
The dataset used in this project collects thousands of confirmed exoplanets and records properties such as distance to the system, stellar magnitude, orbital radius, orbital period, eccentricity, and discovery year, together with a mass multiplier that expresses the planet’s mass relative to Earth or Jupiter. Because these planets were detected with different methods, the catalog contains a wide range of planetary systems: hot Jupiters, warm Neptunes, super-Earths, and long-period cold giants. By treating the mass multiplier as a continuous numerical target, a regression model can learn statistical patterns linking observable orbital and stellar characteristics to typical planetary masses.
Predicting exoplanet mass with machine learning does not replace the detailed physics behind planetary formation or detection techniques. Instead, it uses the idea that planets with similar orbital periods, radii, and host-star properties often have similar masses. Linear regression, for example, fits a function that maps orbital radius, period, eccentricity, and stellar brightness to an approximate mass multiplier. When a new exoplanet is discovered and only some observables are known, the model can provide a fast, rough mass estimate to guide follow-up observations—especially useful when direct radial-velocity measurements are too weak or difficult to obtain.
From a scientific perspective, mass estimates are crucial because they help determine a planet’s density when combined with radius, and therefore its likely composition. High-density planets tend to be rocky, while low-density planets are dominated by thick gaseous envelopes. Knowing how mass, radius, and orbital parameters are distributed across the exoplanet population offers insight into how planetary systems form, how common each type of world is, and where potentially habitable planets might exist. In this project, the predicted mass multiplier serves as a first, data-driven approximation of a planet’s mass—useful for exploration, visualization, and understanding large-scale exoplanet trends.
