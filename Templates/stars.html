Galaxies, Stars & Types

From nuclear fusion to stellar classification
Galaxies are vast systems of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter bound together by gravity. Each galaxy may contain billions or even trillions of stars, ranging from faint red dwarfs to short‑lived blue giants. What makes a star a star is the presence of nuclear fusion in its core: hydrogen nuclei are forced together under extreme temperature and pressure to form helium, releasing enormous amounts of energy. This energy flows outward and balances the inward pull of gravity, creating a stable structure known as hydrostatic equilibrium. Without this balance, a cloud of gas would simply collapse, and a star would never shine for millions or billions of years. [web:83][web:85]
Astronomers classify stars using several key observables: surface temperature, luminosity, radius, and color or spectral type. When these properties are plotted on the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram, most stars fall along a diagonal band called the main sequence, where stars spend the majority of their lifetimes fusing hydrogen into helium. Hot, massive blue stars lie at the upper left with high temperatures and huge luminosities, while small, cool red stars occupy the lower right. Above the main sequence we find giants and supergiants, which are very luminous but often cooler, implying very large radii. Below the main sequence are compact objects such as white dwarfs, which are hot but faint because their radii are much smaller. [web:82][web:84][web:89]
In this project, the dataset includes six broad star types, such as brown dwarfs, red dwarfs, white dwarfs, main‑sequence stars, supergiants, and hypergiants. Brown dwarfs are substellar objects that never sustain stable hydrogen fusion; they are relatively cool and faint, with low luminosities and small radii. Red dwarfs are low‑mass main‑sequence stars that are cooler and less luminous than the Sun, but very common in the universe. White dwarfs are the dense remnants of stars like the Sun, with high temperatures but tiny radii, so they appear hot yet intrinsically faint. Supergiants and hypergiants represent the opposite extreme: they are extremely luminous and often have radii hundreds or thousands of times the Sun’s, placing them high on the HR diagram even when their temperatures are only moderate. [web:88][web:91]
The features in your CSV—temperature, luminosity, radius, and absolute magnitude—are exactly the quantities that astronomers use to distinguish these classes. A star with very high temperature and luminosity is likely to be a massive blue main‑sequence star or a blue supergiant, while one with low temperature and high luminosity must have a very large radius, characteristic of red giants and supergiants. Conversely, an object with high temperature but low luminosity and small radius fits the profile of a white dwarf. By feeding these numerical values into the model, the system effectively recreates the logic of the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram in an automated way: instead of placing the point by hand on a plot, the decision tree uses learned thresholds to assign the most probable type. This is why the same four physical features are sufficient for both the human classification scheme and the machine learning classifier used in this project. [web:82][web:83][web:88]
