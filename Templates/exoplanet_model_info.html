How the Exoplanet Model Works

Linear regression on exoplanet catalog
The exoplanet mass model in this project is a linear regression trained on a cleaned catalog of more than five thousand exoplanets. Each entry in the dataset describes a discovered planet with numerical properties such as distance to its host star, stellar magnitude, orbital radius, orbital period, eccentricity, and discovery year, as well as a target value called the mass multiplier, which encodes the planet’s mass in units of Earth or Jupiter masses. The goal of the model is to learn how these observable quantities relate to the underlying mass so that the mass of new planets can be estimated from similar measurements. [file:93][web:18]
Mathematically, a linear regression model predicts the target value as a weighted sum of the input features plus a constant offset. In this case, the predicted mass multiplier \( \hat{m} \) is modeled as a combination of distance, stellar brightness, orbital parameters, and discovery year, each multiplied by a learned coefficient. Training the model means finding the values of these coefficients that minimize the overall error between predicted and true masses on the training set. The algorithm used (ordinary least squares) searches for the line or hyperplane in feature space that best fits all the points in the sense of minimizing the sum of squared residuals. Because the relationship is linear in the features, it is easy to inspect the learned coefficients and see which variables have the strongest influence on the prediction. [web:99][web:102]
To evaluate the model, the dataset is split into training and test subsets. After learning on the training data, the model is applied to the unseen test exoplanets, and several regression metrics are computed: MAE (mean absolute error), MSE (mean squared error), RMSE (root mean squared error), and R² (coefficient of determination). MAE and RMSE measure the typical size of the prediction error in units of the mass multiplier, while R² indicates how much of the variability in the true masses is explained by the features. Scatter plots of true versus predicted mass, together with residual plots, help visualize these metrics by showing how closely the points follow the ideal one‑to‑one line and whether the residuals are randomly spread around zero. [web:74][web:81]
Although the underlying physics of planet formation and orbital dynamics is complex and often non‑linear, linear regression can still capture the dominant trends present in the catalog. For example, more massive planets are often found at particular ranges of orbital radius and period, and certain detection methods are biased toward specific mass regimes. By exploiting these statistical correlations, the model acts as a fast estimator that can suggest plausible mass values when direct measurements (for example from radial velocity data) are difficult or unavailable. In more advanced research, linear models are often combined with more flexible algorithms such as support vector regression or neural networks, but the linear baseline remains valuable because it is simple to interpret and easy to implement.
